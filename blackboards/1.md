# Blackboard 1 — Functional Equation for Composition-Compatible Weight Functions

## Context
D29 discovery + S288 study. Test whether P4.2's hypothesis (L) is uniquely forced by (C)+(I)+(D).

## 1. Setup

1D free particle. Classical action: \(S_{\mathrm{cl}}(x,y;\Delta t)=\frac{m(y-x)^2}{2\Delta t}\).

General kernel ansatz:
\[
K(x,y;\Delta t) = N(\Delta t)\,W\!\left(\frac{S_{\mathrm{cl}}(x,y;\Delta t)}{\kappa}\right)
\]
\(W\) unknown. Define \(\sigma = S_{\mathrm{cl}}/\kappa = m(y-x)^2/(2\kappa\Delta t)\).

## 2. Composition as Convolution

Semigroup (C): \(K(x,z;T) = \int K(x,y;t_1)\,K(y,z;t_2)\,dy\), \(T=t_1+t_2\).

Set \(K_t(u) \equiv N(t)\,W(mu^2/(2\kappa t))\). Translation invariance gives:
\[
K_T(u) = (K_{t_1}*K_{t_2})(u) \quad\text{(convolution in spatial variable)}
\]

Fourier transform \(\hat K_t(p)=\int K_t(u)\,e^{-ipu}\,du\). Convolution theorem:
\[
\hat K_T(p) = \hat K_{t_1}(p)\,\hat K_{t_2}(p)
\]

## 3. Scaling Reduction

Substitution \(v=u/\sqrt{t}\) in the Fourier integral:
\[
\hat K_t(p) = N(t)\sqrt{t}\;\hat W_0(p\sqrt{t})
\]
where \(\hat W_0(q) = \int W(mv^2/(2\kappa))\,e^{-iqv}\,dv\).

## 4. Functional Equation

Define \(F(p,t) \equiv \log\hat K_t(p) = \log(N(t)\sqrt{t}) + \log\hat W_0(p\sqrt{t})\).

Semigroup: \(F(p,t_1+t_2) = F(p,t_1) + F(p,t_2)\) for all \(p>0\), \(t_1,t_2>0\).

Under continuity, unique solution: \(F(p,t)=t\,\phi(p)\) for some \(\phi\).

Define \(f(q)=\log\hat W_0(q)\), \(g(t)=\log(N(t)\sqrt{t})\). Then:
\[
\boxed{f(p\sqrt{t_1+t_2})+g(t_1+t_2) = f(p\sqrt{t_1})+g(t_1)+f(p\sqrt{t_2})+g(t_2)}
\]

Equivalently: \(f(p\sqrt{t})+g(t) = t\,\phi(p)\) where \(\phi(p)=f(p)+g(1)\).

## 5. Uniqueness Theorem

**Claim.** Under continuity, the unique solution is \(f(q)=\alpha q^2+\beta\).

**Proof.** From \(f(p\sqrt{t})+g(t)=t(f(p)+g(1))\), set \(p=1\):
\[g(t)=t(f(1)+g(1))-f(\sqrt{t})\]

Substitute back: \(f(p\sqrt{t})=t(f(p)-f(1))+f(\sqrt{t})\).

Test \(f(q)=\alpha q^n+\beta\):
- LHS: \(\alpha p^n t^{n/2}+\beta\)
- RHS: \(t(\alpha p^n+\beta-\alpha-\beta)+\alpha t^{n/2}+\beta = \alpha p^n t^{1}+\alpha t^{n/2}-\alpha t+\beta\)

Matching \(q^n\) coefficient: \(t^{n/2}=t^1\) requires \(n=2\).
Matching constant: \(\beta = \alpha t - \alpha t + \beta\). Consistent only for \(n=2\).

Higher-order terms (\(f=\alpha q^2+\gamma q^4+\cdots\)): the \(q^4\) contribution gives
\(\gamma p^4 t^2 \neq \gamma p^4 t + \gamma t^2 - \gamma t\) for all \(p,t\).
Forces \(\gamma=0\). Inductively, all higher coefficients vanish. \(\square\)

## 6. Consequence

\(\hat W_0(q) = e^{\alpha q^2+\beta}\) (Gaussian). Inverse Fourier:
\[
W(mv^2/(2\kappa)) \propto e^{-q_0^2/(4\alpha)}\cdot e^{\text{const}\cdot v^2}
\]
Therefore \(W(\sigma)=C\,e^{c_0\sigma}\) with \(c_0\) determined by \(\alpha\).

**The exponential is the unique composition-compatible weight function** within the class \(K_t(u)=N(t)\,W(S_{\mathrm{cl}}/\kappa)\).

## 7. Alternative Solutions — Systematic Exclusion

| Alternative | \(\hat W_0(q)\) form | Composition? | Reason |
|---|---|---|---|
| \(W=e^{c_0\sigma}\) | \(e^{\alpha q^2+\beta}\) (Gaussian) | YES | \(f=\alpha q^2+\beta\) satisfies functional eq. |
| \(W=\sum a_i e^{c_i\sigma}\) | Sum of Gaussians | NO | \((\sum b_i e^{\alpha_i q^2})^t\) generates cross-widths for \(t\ge2\) |
| \(W=e^{c_0\sigma-\alpha\sigma^2}\) | Non-Gaussian (quartic phase) | NO | \(f(q)\) acquires \(q^4\) term, violates \(n=2\) |
| \(W=(1+\sigma)^n\) | Bessel functions | NO | \(f(q)\neq\alpha q^2+\beta\) |
| \(W=\sigma^\alpha\) | \(|q|^{-(2\alpha+1)}\) | NO | Not Gaussian |
| \(W=\cos(\sigma)\) | Fresnel integrals | NO | Not Gaussian |

## 8. Decomposition of Hypothesis (L)

P4.2 hypothesis (L) = "local exponential weight" decomposes as:
- **(L\_loc)** Kernel depends on \((x,y,\Delta t)\) only through \(S_{\mathrm{cl}}(x,y;\Delta t)\)
- **(L\_exp)** \(W=\exp(c_0\cdot S/\kappa)\)

**Theorem (S288):** (C) + (L\_loc) \(\Rightarrow\) (L\_exp).

(L\_exp) is not an independent assumption — it is a consequence of composition acting on action-dependent kernels.

## 9. Role of (I) and (D)

- **(C) + (L\_loc):** forces \(W=\exp(c_0\sigma)\) (functional form)
- **(D):** forces \([c_0]=[S]^{-1}\), hence \(c_0\propto 1/\kappa\) with \([\kappa]=[\text{action}]\)
- **(I):** fixes normalization \(N(t)=(m/2\pi\kappa t)^{d/2}\), sign of \(c_0\) (convergence), eliminates wrong-sign exponential

## 10. Loophole: Non-Action-Dependent Kernels

If (L\_loc) is dropped (kernel NOT required to depend on \(S_{\mathrm{cl}}\)), composition semigroup alone admits the full Levy-Khintchine class:
\[
\hat K_t(p) = \exp\!\left(t\left[i\mu p - \tfrac{\sigma^2}{2}p^2 + \int(e^{ipx}-1-ipx\mathbf{1}_{|x|<1})\,\nu(dx)\right]\right)
\]
- Gaussian component (\(\sigma^2\) term): the standard quantum propagator
- Levy measure \(\nu\): jump processes (Poisson, Cauchy, stable laws)

These alternatives do NOT have the form \(W(S_{\mathrm{cl}}/\kappa)\). They violate (L\_loc), not (L\_exp).

## 11. Connection to de Gosson / Rivero

- de Gosson's non-uniqueness of quantization: refers to **ordering ambiguity** within the exponential form (different discretizations), NOT to non-exponential weights. Compatible with our uniqueness result.
- Rivero's "convergence conditions force exp(iS/hbar)": consistent — convergence = composition closure + identity limit, which we show forces exponential.

## 12. Verdict

**(L\_exp) is necessary given (C) + (L\_loc).** The exponential form is the **unique** composition-compatible weight function for action-dependent kernels.

The meaningful question for D29 is whether **(L\_loc)** can be weakened — can composition-compatible kernels depend on something other than the classical action? The Levy-Khintchine classification says yes (jump processes), but these are physically distinct theories (not standard quantum mechanics).

**Sharp answer for D29:** Option 1 — (L) is necessary, in the precise sense that (L\_exp) is a theorem of (C)+(L\_loc). The exponential is not assumed; it is derived.
