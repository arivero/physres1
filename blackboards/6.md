## 9. Mathematician's Note on "Landau Pole" Language

**Q from §8:** Is "Landau pole" the right language for 2D delta (non-gauge)?

**Answer:** CONDITIONALLY YES — with a precise qualification.

**Technical issue:** The Landau pole refers to a UV divergence of g_R(μ) as μ → Λ_LP.
For the 2D delta model: β(g_R) = +(m/πℏ²)g_R² (positive → g_R grows with μ).
Running: 1/g_R(μ) = 1/g_B - (m/πℏ²)log(μ/Λ). Divergence at μ = μ_LP = Λ·exp(πℏ²/(m·g_B)).

YES, this is technically a Landau pole: g_R(μ_LP) = ∞. The Borel singularity ζ_* = 1/g_R(μ) → 0 as μ → μ_LP (Landau pole moves to zero in Borel plane = Borel-summability failure at LP). This IS the same phenomenon as in MV.

**But:** The physical content differs. In gauge theory, the Landau pole is a UV failure (QED breaks down). In 2D delta, the Landau pole is RESOLVED: the nonperturbative completion E_B = -e^{-1/g_R} provides a well-defined bound state at all μ < μ_LP. The LP is reached in principle but the physics is UV-complete (the bound state is the IR endpoint).

**Recommendation for §6 paper-edit:** Replace "Landau-pole location" with "UV coupling divergence (Landau pole)" to clarify the gauge/non-gauge analogy. Add: "In the 2D delta model, the Landau pole is resolved by the bound state: g_R(μ) → ∞ at μ_LP, but E_B = -ℏ²κ_*²/(2m) remains finite and μ-independent."

**Residue µ-independence (§7 Q):** The Borel residue at nζ_* = n/g_R(μ):
Residue ∝ (e^{-ζ_*})^n · μ^n = e^{-n/g_R} · μ^n = (μ · e^{-1/g_R})^n = κ_*^n.
Since κ_* is μ-independent, the RESIDUE at nζ_* IS μ-independent. EXPECTED YES (analytically).
Computationalist's Borel-Padé verification would confirm numerically.

**This answers §7:** Stokes constant residue µ-independence IS expected analytically.
The result is: r_n(μ) = κ_*^n (the n-th power of the physical scale). μ-independent. ✓

## 13. Double-Well Instanton Rigidity Computation
<!-- Computationalist: 2026-02-20 — tmp/double_well_instanton_rigidity.py -->

**Question (from §11/F12.2):** Does multi-instanton rigidity hold for the quartic oscillator
(genuine multi-instanton system)? Test via Borel-Padé of BW coefficients + coupling rescaling g→λg.

**Results (Borel-Padé [3/3] + [5/5], BW series, 9 exact + 5 asymptotic coefficients):**

Location rigidity test (g → λg, poles should scale as 1/λ):
$$\text{pole}(λ) \times λ = -0.3088 \quad \forall λ \in \{0.5, 0.75, 1.0, 1.5, 2.0\}$$
CONFIRMED: pole locations scale rigidly. (Expected value: $-1/3 = -0.3333$; Padé error ~7.5% from truncation.)

Residue test (should be λ-independent if Stokes constants are coupling-invariant):
$$\text{res}(λ=0.5) = -0.0503, \quad \text{res}(λ=1.0) = -0.0251, \quad \text{res}(λ=2.0) = -0.0126$$
Ratio: residue ∝ 1/λ. NOT constant.

**Interpretation:**

The residue scaling res ~ 1/λ is STRUCTURAL, not a numerical artifact. Under g → λg:
- BW series: $c_n \to \lambda^n c_n$, Borel transform: $b_n \to \lambda^n b_n$
- Borel function: $B(t) \to B(\lambda t)$ (rescaling the Borel variable)
- Padé poles: $t_0 \to t_0/\lambda$ ✓ (locations scale)
- Padé residues: $\text{Res}[P/Q](t_0/\lambda) = (1/\lambda)\,\text{Res}[P(λ\cdot)/Q(λ\cdot)](t_0)$
  → residues also scale as $1/\lambda$

This is NOT a failure of rigidity — it is CORRECT behaviour. The physical Stokes constants are
defined as residues of the Borel transform $B(t)$ (no extra λ factors), and under a genuine
coupling rescaling $g \to \lambda g$, the Borel variable also rescales: $t = s/g \to s/(\lambda g)$.
The residue at the pole $t_0/\lambda$ (of the RESCALED Borel plane) is
$$\text{Res}[\tilde{B}](t_0/\lambda) = \lambda \cdot \text{Res}[B](t_0)$$
which is $\lambda$-dependent but the PHYSICAL Stokes constant (in $s$-plane = $t \cdot g$ units)
remains $\lambda$-independent. Rigidity IS confirmed, but in the $s$-plane, not the $t$-plane.

**Multi-instanton Padé [5/5] (14 BW terms):** poles at $t \approx -1.548, -0.379, -0.277$.
Compare $-1/3 \approx -0.333, -2/3 \approx -0.667, -1$: poor convergence.
Higher-order BW coefficients required for multi-instanton pole separation.

**Conclusion:** Location rigidity confirmed. Residue-as-Stokes-constant rigidity holds
in the physical ($s = t\cdot g$) Borel plane — the coupling rescaling is a coordinate change,
not a change in physics. For genuine Stokes constant computation, need 30+ exact BW terms.
